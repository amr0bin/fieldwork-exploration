# Fieldwork: Accessibility, Disability Justice, and Emergent Learning
### An exploration repository — Accessible Learning Labs

---

## What this is

This repository is a thinking space.

It holds the structural foundations of *Fieldwork* — a 24-week curriculum for inclusive learning designers built around a working hypothesis: that sustainable, effective learning only becomes possible when three things work together simultaneously.

- **Accessibility** as the essential infrastructure — the legal and technical baseline that removes barriers before they occur
- **Disability Justice** as the ethical and political compass — centring intersectionality, the leadership of those most impacted, and collective access over individual accommodation
- **Emergent Learning** as the pedagogical method — treating knowledge as something that grows relationally, contextually, and iteratively, in community

The hypothesis is that you cannot have one of these without the other two. Most learning systems try. That gap between policy and practice is what this project investigates.

## The structural recipe — how to use it

The `structure.html` file contains the recipe I return to week by week as I write.

Each week follows the same four-part pattern:

1. **Heading** — a provocation, not a topic label. Something that could be wrong, or that challenges an assumption a reader is likely to hold. The argument starts here.

2. **Subheadings** — three moves, one per pillar:
   - What the *system currently does or requires* (Accessibility / infrastructure)
   - *Whose interests that serves and who it misses* (Disability Justice / ethics)
   - *What becomes possible when the design is made adaptive and relational* (Emergent Learning / method)

3. **Keywords** — 3 to 5 conceptual anchors that carry the week's intellectual weight and connect it to the broader hypothesis. These recur across weeks in different combinations — that repetition is how cumulative understanding builds.

4. **Key sentences** — one load-bearing claim per subheading. The sentence you'd underline. True at the week's specific level and also true at the level of the whole hypothesis.

When I sit down to write Part 1 or Part 2 of any given week's Substack post, these four elements are what I write toward or write away from. They are not the writing — they are the structure the writing hangs on.

## The Substack arc — what the writing produces

Each of the 24 weeks generates two posts:

**Part 1 — Why, See**
Grounds the week's principles with a real-world success case from somewhere in the world. The "Why" explains the tension the week is working with. The "See" examines how a real organization, community, or program navigated it — not as a perfect model, but as a site of learning.

**Part 2 — Try, Share, Reflect**
Gives readers practical activities to try in their own contexts — with coworkers, peers, friends — and invites them to bring what they find back into community. This is where emergent learning actually happens: context-driven, relational, iterative.

48 pieces total. 24 cases. 24 practice activities. The structure stays constant; the content adapts to each week's specific territory.

## The working hypothesis — in one place

> Sustainable and effective learning systems are only possible when they move beyond mere legal compliance (accessibility) to embody a radical, intersectional framework (disability justice) that facilitates continuous, context-driven adaptation (emergent learning).
>
> The gap between policy and practice in inclusive learning exists because most institutions are trying to solve with one pillar what requires all three. Knowledge only travels as far as the systems designed to carry it — and most systems were not designed to carry everyone.
