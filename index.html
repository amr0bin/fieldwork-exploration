Fieldwork:
Accessibility, Disability Justice,
and Emergent Learning

A working curriculum for inclusive learning designers — exploring the synthesis of legal compliance, intersectional ethics, and context-driven pedagogy.

Sustainable and effective learning systems are only possible when they move beyond mere legal compliance to embody a radical, intersectional framework that facilitates continuous, context-driven adaptation.

The framework

Three pillars, one hypothesis

Knowledge only travels as far as the systems designed to carry it. The Fieldwork hypothesis integrates three mutually reinforcing pillars.

Pillar 1

Accessibility

The essential infrastructure. Removes physical, technological, and attitudinal barriers to ensure equitable participation.

  • Accessible Canada Act (ACA), AODA, ADA
  • WCAG 2.1 — perceivable, operable, understandable, robust
  • Proactive design over reactive retrofitting
  • Diverse formats: braille, captions, text-to-speech
Pillar 2

Disability Justice

The ethical and political compass. Critiques narrow disability rights frameworks and centres intersectional leadership.

  • Intersectionality — race, gender, class, sexuality
  • Leadership of those most impacted
  • Collective access over individual accommodation
  • Interdependence over independence as the goal
Pillar 3

Emergent Learning

The pedagogical method. Treats learning as a dynamic, relational, and context-driven habit rather than a transfer of information.

  • Roots in Paulo Freire and critical theory
  • Collaborative inquiry and peer-based adaptation
  • Iterative experimentation and real-time feedback
  • Conditions that facilitate emergence

24-week structure

The curriculum

Four phases move the designer from consumer of information to practitioner-researcher. Each phase builds on the last.

Foundations & thesis discovery
Goal: Align personal interest with the thesis and establish the research problem
Weeks 1–6
Week 1
Personal interest & problem identification
Reflect on personal strengths and identify a high-stakes environment where the gap between policy and practice is evident.
Week 2
Deconstructing the hypothesis
Analyze the three pillars: accessibility, disability justice, and emergent learning as interconnected and mutually dependent.
Week 3
Preliminary research
Conduct initial scans of the selected environment to understand current systems designed to carry knowledge.
Week 4
Developing the research question
Narrow the focus from the broad hypothesis to a specific, researchable question grounded in your environment.
Week 5
Evaluating feasibility
Assess available resources, time constraints, and ethical implications for the full 24-week investigation.
Week 6
Developing the research proposal
Outline research objectives, methodology, and the significance of the study as a blueprint for the phases ahead.
Literature review & theoretical scaffolding
Goal: Situate the inquiry within existing legal, social, and pedagogical frameworks
Weeks 7–12
Week 7
Legal frameworks
Review the Accessible Canada Act, AODA, and ADA to understand the baseline of legal compliance.
Week 8
Technical standards — WCAG 2.1
Deep dive into WCAG 2.1 principles: perceivability, operability, understandability, and robustness.
Week 9
Disability justice — the compass
Study intersectionality, leadership of those most impacted, and interdependence. Contrast the social model with medical models.
Week 10
Emergent learning — the method
Explore the roots of emergent learning in critical theory and Paulo Freire's work, focusing on context-driven adaptation.
Week 11
Neuroeducation & brain health
Investigate how active learning, spaced repetition, and emotional engagement impact cognitive function and neuroplasticity.
Week 12
Identifying research gaps
Synthesize the literature to find where current inclusive design fails to integrate all three pillars simultaneously.
Investigation & methodology
Goal: Conduct field research to gather qualitative and quantitative data
Weeks 13–18
Week 13
Choosing methodology
Select a mixed-methods approach to balance numerical data with rich contextual insights from your environment.
Week 14
Stakeholder engagement
Identify and consult diverse stakeholders, including disabled learners and community members, to ensure inclusive consultation.
Week 15
Qualitative data collection
Conduct in-depth interviews or focus groups with marginalized learners to understand lived experiences and barriers.
Week 16
Quantitative data collection
Deploy surveys to measure broader trends in accessibility and engagement within the target system.
Week 17
Ethical oversight
Ensure ongoing informed consent, confidentiality, and anonymity for all participants throughout the research process.
Week 18
Collaborative inquiry
Facilitate a peer-based session to share preliminary findings and foster cross-pollination of ideas across contexts.
Analysis, design & synthesis
Goal: Translate findings into inclusive learning content and finalize the thesis
Weeks 19–24
Week 19
Data analysis
Use thematic analysis for qualitative data and statistical methods for quantitative data to derive meaningful conclusions.
Week 20
Cross-case analysis
Compare findings across different learner groups to reveal broader insights applicable to the system as a whole.
Week 21
Backward design for learning content
Start with the end goals — social transformation and resilience — to design the final learning intervention.
Week 22
Prototype development
Apply Universal Design for Learning (UDL) and proactive design principles to create the learning content artifact.
Week 23
Iteration & feedback
Seek feedback from advisors, peers, and original stakeholders to refine the content toward its final form.
Week 24
Culmination & dissemination
Finalize the comprehensive body of work — beyond compliance, toward emergent, intersectional learning for others.

Core competencies

Designer development

Ten core competencies

The competencies an inclusive learning designer develops across the 24-week arc — mapped to the three pillars of the Fieldwork hypothesis.

01
Methodological inquiry & research formulation
Research

Narrowing broad interests into specific, researchable questions. Selecting qualitative, quantitative, or mixed-methods approaches that align with the question.

02
Technical compliance & standards application
Accessibility

Implementing WCAG 2.1 alongside federal and provincial legislation (ACA, AODA) to ensure the legal and technical infrastructure is met.

03
Proactive & universal design
Accessibility

Moving beyond reactive retrofitting to integrate accessibility from the outset using UDL — multiple means of representation, action & expression, and engagement.

04
Intersectional analysis
Disability justice

Recognizing that learners experience overlapping forms of oppression. Applying an intersectional lens and centring the leadership of those most impacted by systemic injustices.

05
Collaborative stakeholder engagement
Disability justice

Actively involving diverse stakeholders — students, faculty, community members — to ensure materials are relevant and reflective of lived experience.

06
Facilitating emergent adaptation
Emergent learning

Treating learning as a dynamic, relational, context-driven process. Fostering conditions where iterative experimentation allows the system to respond to real-world challenges.

07
Neuroeducational literacy
Emergent learning

Understanding neuroplasticity to create environments that stimulate neural growth. Embedding active learning, spaced repetition, and emotional engagement into curriculum design.

08
Iterative reflective practice
Research

Engaging in both reflection-on-action (after events) and reflection-in-action (during activities). Continuous revision through peer reviews and advisor feedback cycles.

09
Backward curriculum design
Emergent learning

Starting with desired outcomes and working backward to define evidence of mastery and instructional activities. Every curriculum component directly aligned with the final objectives.

10
Cultivating collective access & interdependence
Disability justice

Valuing interdependence — community support and mutual aid — over independence as the sole goal. Designing for collective access so learner needs are integrated, not treated as exceptions.
