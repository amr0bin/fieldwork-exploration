The structural recipe

For each of the 24 weeks: the heading provocation, three subheadings mirroring the three pillars, the conceptual keywords, and the load-bearing key sentences. This is the skeleton that holds the writing together.

How to read each week
1
Heading
The provocation — an argument starter, not a topic label
2
Subheadings
Three moves: infrastructure → ethics → method, mirroring the three pillars
3
Keywords
3–5 conceptual anchors that recur across weeks to build cumulative understanding
4
Key sentences
One load-bearing claim per subheading — true at week level and hypothesis level
Phase 1 — Foundations & thesis discovery
Align personal interest with the thesis and establish the research problem
Week 1

The gap between policy and practice is not an accident — it is a design choice.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What the system claims to do for all learners
  • Disability Justice
    Who the system was actually designed for
  • Emergent Learning
    What becomes possible when you start from your own context
Keywords
high-stakes environment policy-practice gap personal interest systems of knowledge problem identification
Key sentences
Accessibility
Most learning systems can describe their accessibility commitments in policy but cannot demonstrate them in practice.
Disability Justice
The environment you work in is not neutral — it was designed with particular bodies, minds, and experiences in mind, and those choices are still operating.
Emergent Learning
Your context is not just where you will apply the learning — it is the primary research site, and your own curiosity is the first instrument.
Week 2

Accessibility without justice is compliance. Justice without method is aspiration. You need all three.

Subheadings

  • Disability Justice

  • Emergent Learning

Keywords

Key sentences
Accessibility
Accessibility legislation sets a legal floor — the minimum a system must do — but a floor is not a destination.
Disability Justice
Disability justice asks who was in the room when the floor was set, and whose exclusion was written into the standard itself.
Emergent Learning
Emergent learning is the practice that holds both pillars in motion — it transforms static compliance and ethical commitment into a living, adaptive system.
Week 3

Knowledge only travels as far as the systems designed to carry it — and most systems were not designed to carry everyone.

  • Emergent Learning

Key sentences
Accessibility
Every learning environment has a de facto delivery system — the question is whether it was designed intentionally or inherited by default.
Disability Justice
The gaps in a system are not random; they trace the boundaries of whose participation was assumed from the start.
Emergent Learning
Preliminary research is not passive — it is the first act of inquiry, and what you notice first tells you something important about where your attention has been trained.
Week 4

A research question is not a topic — it is a commitment to look at something specific enough to actually see it.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility

  • Disability Justice
    Whose experience should the question be designed to surface
  • Emergent Learning
    How a well-framed question opens the inquiry rather than closing it
Keywords
research question narrowing focus collective access framing researchable scope
Key sentences
Accessibility
A researchable question must be specific enough to test within a real environment, with real constraints — not broad enough to be true everywhere and therefore verifiable nowhere.
Disability Justice
The best research questions in inclusive design are asked on behalf of the people most systematically excluded from the existing research — naming them in the question is not rhetorical, it is methodological.
Emergent Learning
A well-framed question does not predict its answer — it creates the conditions under which an unexpected answer can surface and be taken seriously.
Week 5

Ethical constraints are not obstacles to good research — they are part of the design.

Subheadings

  • Disability Justice

  • Emergent Learning

Key sentences
Accessibility
A 24-week investigation requires honest accounting of what you actually have access to — the research will be shaped by those realities whether you name them or not.
Disability Justice
When your research participants include people from marginalized communities, informed consent is not a form to be signed — it is an ongoing relationship to be maintained.
Emergent Learning
Acknowledging your constraints early does not narrow the inquiry — it focuses it, and focused inquiry produces more honest findings than unconstrained ambition.
Week 6

Subheadings
  • Accessibility

  • Disability Justice

  • Emergent Learning
    How a living blueprint stays responsive as the research unfolds
Keywords
research proposal blueprint accountability iterative design significance
Key sentences
Accessibility
A strong proposal names its objectives clearly enough that anyone reading it could assess whether the investigation actually achieved them.
Disability Justice
The significance of inclusive design research is not measured by its contribution to the literature alone — it is measured by what it changes for the people who were the subject of the inquiry.
Emergent Learning
The proposal is a starting position, not a contract — the capacity to revise it in response to what you actually find is not weakness, it is intellectual honesty.
Phase 2 — Literature review & theoretical scaffolding
Situate the inquiry within existing legal, social, and pedagogical frameworks
Week 7

The law tells you the minimum. It does not tell you what inclusion actually feels like from the inside.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What ACA, AODA, and ADA actually mandate — and what they leave open
  • Disability Justice
    Who shaped these laws and whose experience informed their limits
  • Emergent Learning
    How legal literacy becomes a tool for design, not just compliance
Keywords
Accessible Canada Act AODA ADA legal baseline rights-based approach
Key sentences
Accessibility
The ACA, AODA, and ADA define the legal floor — what federally regulated and provincially mandated organizations must do — but compliance with these laws does not guarantee an accessible experience.
Disability Justice
Disability rights legislation was shaped by advocacy movements that were often predominantly white and focused on physical disability — the laws carry those assumptions into their standards.
Emergent Learning
Legal literacy is not the end of inclusive design — it is the beginning, and the most useful question is not "does this comply?" but "who does this still fail?"
Week 8

Technical standards describe what accessible content must do — but a checklist cannot feel what a learner experiences.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    WCAG 2.1's four principles: perceivable, operable, understandable, robust
  • Disability Justice
    What WCAG compliance still cannot account for in lived experience
  • Emergent Learning
    How technical standards become a design starting point, not an endpoint
Keywords
WCAG 2.1 perceivability operability understandability robustness
Key sentences
Accessibility
WCAG 2.1 provides the technical vocabulary for accessible digital content — perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust — and every learning designer must know it fluently.
Disability Justice
A document can pass every WCAG criterion and still be emotionally inaccessible, culturally alienating, or pedagogically useless for the learner it was meant to serve.
Emergent Learning
Technical standards are the necessary grammar of accessibility — but grammar alone does not make meaning, and meaning is what learning requires.
Week 9

Disability justice does not begin where disability rights ends — it begins where disability rights refuses to look.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What the social model of disability changed — and what it still misses
  • Disability Justice
    Intersectionality, leadership of those most impacted, and interdependence
  • Emergent Learning
    How disability justice reframes the designer's role and relationship to learners
Keywords
disability justice intersectionality social model interdependence leadership of those most impacted
Key sentences
Accessibility
The social model of disability — which locates the problem in society rather than in the person — was a radical reframe, but it has been critiqued for treating disability as a single-axis identity.
Disability Justice
We do not live single-issue lives: a disabled person who is also Black, queer, and working-class experiences a compounded, intersectional form of exclusion that neither disability rights nor anti-racism fully names.
Emergent Learning
Disability justice asks the designer to move from expert to co-learner — the people most impacted by a system must lead its redesign, because their knowledge of what fails is irreplaceable.
Week 10

Learning is not a product delivered to a learner — it is a process that happens between people, in context, over time.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    How traditional education models structure the delivery of knowledge
  • Disability Justice
    Freire's critique: whose knowledge counts, and who gets to ask why
  • Emergent Learning
    What emergent learning looks like when it is working
Keywords
emergent learning Paulo Freire critical pedagogy context-driven collaborative inquiry
Key sentences
Accessibility
Traditional education models treat learning as a transfer of information from expert to novice — a structure that is efficient for delivering content but poorly suited to building understanding.
Disability Justice
Freire's "banking concept" of education — where learners are passive recipients of a teacher's deposits — is not just pedagogically limited, it is politically loaded: it decides whose knowledge has value before the learner enters the room.
Emergent Learning
Emergent learning treats knowledge as something that arises in the interaction between people and their context — which means the designer's job is to create conditions, not to control outcomes.
Week 11

The brain does not store information the way a hard drive does — and most curriculum design pretends it does.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What cognitive science tells us about how learning becomes memory
  • Disability Justice
    How neurological diversity challenges the "standard" learner assumption
  • Emergent Learning
    Active learning, spaced repetition, and emotional engagement as design principles
Keywords
neuroplasticity spaced repetition active learning emotional engagement neuroeducation
Key sentences
Accessibility
Neuroeducation demonstrates that memory formation requires active engagement, spaced repetition, and emotional relevance — none of which is guaranteed by content delivery alone.
Disability Justice
The "standard" learner assumed by most curriculum design is neurotypical by default — and every accommodation added afterward is evidence of a design that failed before it started.
Emergent Learning
When a learning environment stimulates neural growth through active participation and emotional connection, it is not just being more engaging — it is being more accessible.
Week 12

Most inclusive design has one of the three pillars. The gap in the literature is the synthesis.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    Where the accessibility literature stops short of justice or pedagogy
  • Disability Justice
    Where disability justice frameworks lack practical learning methodology
  • Emergent Learning
    How identifying the gap becomes the research contribution
Keywords
research gaps literature synthesis integration failure gap statement three-pillar synthesis
Key sentences
Accessibility
The accessibility literature is technically sophisticated but often ethically thin — it tells you what to build without asking who was consulted in the building.
Disability Justice
The disability justice literature is ethically rich but rarely translates its principles into specific, replicable pedagogical practices — the compass points, but does not always map the path.
Emergent Learning
The gap that matters — the one this body of work addresses — is not missing information but missing integration: no existing framework holds all three pillars together as a functioning system.
Phase 3 — Investigation & methodology
Conduct field research to gather qualitative and quantitative data
Week 13

The method you choose is a statement about what kind of knowledge you believe counts.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What mixed methods offer that single approaches cannot
  • Disability Justice
    Why quantitative data alone cannot capture what exclusion feels like
  • Emergent Learning
    How your method shapes what can emerge from the inquiry
Keywords
mixed methods qualitative research quantitative research methodological alignment triangulation
Key sentences
Accessibility
A mixed-methods approach balances the measurable breadth of quantitative data with the contextual depth of qualitative insight — both are necessary for understanding a complex system.
Disability Justice
Numbers can show that a gap exists; only lived-experience narratives can show what it costs, and both forms of evidence are necessary to make the argument for change.
Emergent Learning
The method is not neutral — choosing to listen to marginalized learners in depth is itself an act of inclusive design, because it treats their experience as evidence rather than anecdote.
Week 14

You cannot design inclusively for people you have not genuinely consulted — and consultation is not the same as asking permission.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    Who counts as a stakeholder in an inclusive learning system
  • Disability Justice
    The difference between consultation and co-design
  • Emergent Learning
    How genuine stakeholder engagement changes what you find
Keywords
stakeholder engagement inclusive consultation co-design lived experience community voice
Key sentences
Accessibility
Inclusive consultation requires deliberately seeking out the stakeholders who are least likely to appear in official feedback channels — because their absence from those channels is itself evidence of a design failure.
Disability Justice
Co-design is not asking disabled learners to validate decisions already made — it is inviting them into the process before the decisions exist, and accepting that their presence will change the design.
Emergent Learning
When the people most affected by a system are consulted as full participants in its redesign, the inquiry becomes something different — it becomes collective, and collective knowledge is more resilient than individual expertise.
Week 15

Listening is a research method — and most of us were never taught to do it without an agenda.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What qualitative methods — interviews, focus groups — make visible
  • Disability Justice
    How to listen to marginalized experience without extracting it
  • Emergent Learning
    What open-ended listening reveals that structured questions cannot
Keywords
qualitative data in-depth interviews focus groups lived experience extractive research
Key sentences
Accessibility
In-depth interviews and focus groups give marginalized learners the space to describe their experience in their own terms — which is often very different from the terms the system uses to describe them.
Disability Justice
Extractive research takes stories from communities and gives back publications — genuinely inclusive research asks what the community needs from the process, and builds that into the design.
Emergent Learning
The most important data in an inclusive design inquiry often arrives unexpectedly — which is why the researcher must be trained to listen for what they did not think to ask about.
Week 16

Numbers make the invisible visible at scale — but only if you ask questions that allow the truth to show up.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What surveys can measure about accessibility and engagement across a system
  • Disability Justice
    How survey design can perpetuate the very biases it claims to measure
  • Emergent Learning
    Using quantitative data as a provocation rather than a conclusion
Keywords
quantitative data surveys sampling measurement bias systemic trends
Key sentences
Accessibility
Surveys allow you to measure patterns across a population that qualitative methods alone cannot reach — they reveal the scale of a problem even when individual stories have been overlooked.
Disability Justice
A survey designed without input from the communities it targets will measure what the designer assumed was important — and systematically miss what the community knows is actually going on.
Emergent Learning
Quantitative findings are most useful when treated as questions, not answers — the number that surprises you is an invitation to go back into the community and ask why.
Week 17

Informed consent is not a form — it is a relationship, and it has to be renegotiated as the research changes.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What ethical research standards require at every stage of inquiry
  • Disability Justice
    Why marginalized participants require deeper ethical accountability
  • Emergent Learning
    Ethics as an ongoing practice woven into the research, not a checkpoint at the start
Keywords
informed consent confidentiality anonymity ethical oversight ongoing accountability
Key sentences
Accessibility
Ethical research requires informed consent, confidentiality, and anonymity as baseline conditions — not optional additions — for every participant at every stage.
Disability Justice
When research participants belong to communities that have historically been exploited by researchers, the ethical obligation extends beyond compliance — it requires transparency about how the findings will be used and who will benefit.
Emergent Learning
As the research evolves and unexpected directions emerge, ethical oversight must evolve with it — the initial consent form cannot anticipate everything the inquiry will require.
Week 18

Sharing preliminary findings is not a risk to manage — it is a research method that makes the findings better.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What a peer-based inquiry session looks like in practice
  • Disability Justice
    Whose perspectives must be present in the room for the analysis to be trustworthy
  • Emergent Learning
    How cross-pollination of ideas strengthens both the findings and the community
Keywords
collaborative inquiry peer-based learning cross-pollination collective sensemaking preliminary findings
Key sentences
Accessibility
A collaborative inquiry session creates a structured space for preliminary findings to be examined by multiple perspectives before they harden into conclusions.
Disability Justice
If the people most affected by the system are not present when the findings are analyzed, the analysis will reflect the assumptions of whoever is in the room — and that is a validity problem, not just an ethical one.
Emergent Learning
When peers from different contexts engage with the same preliminary findings, the patterns that survive across those conversations are the ones most likely to be real.
Phase 4 — Analysis, design & synthesis
Translate findings into inclusive learning content and finalize the thesis
Week 19

Analysis is not the moment you discover what your data means — it is the moment you decide what questions to bring to it.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    Thematic and statistical methods for making sense of mixed data
  • Disability Justice
    How to analyze without flattening the complexity of lived experience
  • Emergent Learning
    Holding findings lightly enough that unexpected meaning can surface
Keywords
thematic analysis statistical methods data interpretation reliability validity
Key sentences
Accessibility
Thematic analysis surfaces recurring patterns in qualitative data; statistical methods identify relationships in quantitative data — used together, they produce findings that are both deep and broad.
Disability Justice
Flattening complex lived experience into a clean finding is its own form of exclusion — analysis must preserve enough specificity that the people whose stories generated the data would still recognize themselves in the conclusions.
Emergent Learning
The most important analytical move is staying curious about the finding that doesn't fit — because the outlier is usually where the system's real logic is hiding.
Week 20

When the same pattern appears across different contexts, it stops being a local problem and starts being a systemic one.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What cross-case analysis reveals about systemic accessibility failures
  • Disability Justice
    How shared patterns of exclusion name structural, not individual, problems
  • Emergent Learning
    What comparing across contexts makes possible that single-case analysis cannot
Keywords
cross-case analysis systemic patterns comparative analysis transferability broader insights
Key sentences
Accessibility
Cross-case analysis compares findings across different learner groups and environments to identify patterns that transcend any single context — and those patterns are the most actionable findings.
Disability Justice
When disabled learners in different institutions describe the same barrier in different words, it is no longer an individual experience — it is evidence of a structural problem that no individual accommodation can fix.
Emergent Learning
The insight that holds across multiple different contexts is the one most likely to generate a learning design that can adapt — because it was born from diversity rather than from a single case.
Week 21

If you do not know what transformation looks like at the end, you cannot design the path toward it.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    How backward design ensures every element serves the learning goal
  • Disability Justice
    Defining "transformation" and "resilience" in terms of the learners most impacted
  • Emergent Learning
    Working backward from social change as a design constraint
Keywords
backward design end goals social transformation resilience learning outcomes
Key sentences
Accessibility
Backward design begins with the end — what should learners be able to do, understand, and embody — and works backward to ensure that every instructional choice serves that destination.
Disability Justice
Defining transformation in terms of the most marginalized learner — not the average or the most able — produces a learning system that genuinely includes rather than one that accommodates after the fact.
Emergent Learning
When social transformation is the explicit goal of a learning design, every content choice, every facilitation decision, and every assessment becomes answerable to a question more demanding than "is this correct?" — namely, "does this change anything?"
Week 22

A prototype is not a polished product — it is a question made physical enough to test with real people.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    Applying UDL principles — representation, action, engagement — to the prototype
  • Disability Justice
    Why the first test must involve the people the design is for
  • Emergent Learning
    What a rough prototype teaches you that planning cannot
Keywords
prototype Universal Design for Learning proactive design user testing multiple means
Key sentences
Accessibility
UDL's three principles — multiple means of representation, action and expression, and engagement — must be applied from the first draft of the prototype, not retrofitted into a finished product.
Disability Justice
The first person to test the prototype should be someone who would typically be excluded by the default version — because their experience will reveal the design assumptions that were invisible to the designer.
Emergent Learning
A rough prototype in the hands of a real learner generates more honest design feedback in one session than months of planning — imperfection is not a failure of the prototype, it is its function.
Week 23

Feedback is not the end of design — it is the design continuing through other people's eyes.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    How structured feedback processes improve clarity and usability
  • Disability Justice
    Whose feedback has been missing from inclusive design processes — and why
  • Emergent Learning
    Reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action as iterative design practices
Keywords
iteration peer review reflection-on-action reflection-in-action refinement
Key sentences
Accessibility
Structured feedback from advisors, peers, and original stakeholders identifies gaps in clarity, coherence, and accessibility that the designer, too close to their own work, cannot see.
Disability Justice
If the stakeholders who were consulted in the research phase are not also consulted in the feedback phase, the final design risks betraying the insights that were trusted to you.
Emergent Learning
Donald Schön's distinction between reflection-on-action (after) and reflection-in-action (during) describes two different kinds of learning — and a mature designer practices both, using feedback as the trigger for each.
Week 24

The work is only complete when it can be used by someone who was not in the room when it was made.

Subheadings
  • Accessibility
    What a final learning system must demonstrate about accessibility
  • Disability Justice
    How the final work embodies collective access and intersectional learning
  • Emergent Learning
    Dissemination as the beginning of the next cycle of emergence
Keywords
culmination dissemination collective access relationality practitioner-researcher
Key sentences
Accessibility
The final body of work must be itself accessible — in format, in language, in the assumptions it makes about its reader — or it will reproduce the very exclusions it set out to address.
Disability Justice
A work that embodies collective access does not summarize the community's experience for an academic audience — it returns something usable to the community that generated the knowledge.
Emergent Learning
Dissemination is not the end of the learning — it is the moment when the work enters a wider community and begins to adapt through contact with contexts its designer never anticipated, which is emergence continuing.
