Note
The pillar tag names which of the three pillars this week's post primarily draws from. Some weeks will draw from more than one — name the dominant one. This tag helps readers who are following a particular thread navigate the archive.
Heading The provocation An argument starter, not a topic label
Source
Drawn directly from the structural recipe for this week. It is a statement or question that could be wrong, or that challenges an assumption the reader is likely to hold.
Opening line
One sentence immediately below the heading that names what is at stake for the reader personally. Not a summary of the post — a reason to keep reading. It should speak directly to someone already doing this work.
Heading: from the structural recipe · Opening line: 1 sentence
Why The conceptual foundation The tension → the reframe → the key sentence
What this section does
Opens with the tension — what the field currently does or assumes, and why that is insufficient. Introduces the concept that reframes it. Closes with the week's key sentence from the structural recipe, stated or closely paraphrased. The reader should feel taken seriously, not lectured at.
Three moves
Move 1 — The tension
Name what the system currently does or requires (the accessibility / infrastructure dimension). Be specific — a reader should recognise their own context in it.
Move 2 — The reframe
Introduce the concept that challenges that assumption (the disability justice / ethics dimension). This is where the week's intellectual work happens.
Move 3 — The key sentence
Close with the load-bearing claim from the structural recipe (the emergent learning / method dimension). This is the sentence the reader will carry out of the post.
250–350 words · 2–3 paragraphs
See The real-world case Context → what they did → what changed → what it reveals
Introduction
One sentence that names the case — where, who, what conditions. This orients the reader before the four-part structure begins.
Four-part structure
Context
Name the environment, the population, the specific challenge or gap. Be specific enough that readers can locate themselves in relation to it — either "this is my context" or "this is adjacent to my context." 2–4 sentences.
What they did
Describe the design decision, intervention, or practice. Focus on the choices made, not just the outcome. What did they prioritise? What did they refuse to do? 2–4 sentences.
What changed
The observable outcome — what was measurably or visibly different. Be honest about limits: what changed and what did not. 2–4 sentences.
What it reveals
Connect the case back to the week's concept. What does this example make visible about the hypothesis that a general argument alone could not? This is the sentence a reader will remember. 2–3 sentences.
Case selection principles
Vary geography and sector across the 24 weeks — not all higher education, not all North American, not all the same scale. The range of cases is itself a demonstration of the emergent, intersectional thesis. Adapt the four-part structure as the case demands — some cases will need more on context, others on what changed.

Same heading as Part 1
Repeat the week's provocation heading — it holds both posts together as one unit. Follow immediately with one sentence that reconnects to last week's concept and signals the shift: from understanding to action. Something like: "Last week we looked at why [X]. This week we put it to work."
Try The practical activity Concept moving into the reader's hands
Opening paragraph
One short paragraph that names the skill or practice and connects it directly to the week's concept. Why this activity, why now, why it matters in the reader's specific context. Respect their time from the first sentence.
The activity — step by step
1
Written for an early-career practitioner who has never done this before. Plain language, no jargon.
2
No assumed tools or institutional resources — adaptable to any context and any level of access.
3
Each step is a complete action, not a category. "Write down three..." not "Consider..."
4
3–6 steps total. If it takes more than 6, it is two activities — split it or simplify it.
Activity metadata
Time required: [X minutes] What you need: [minimal list] Adapting for your context: [one sentence]
Activity section: 200–300 words
Watch The companion video Standalone on YouTube · companion in the post
What the video does
Demonstrates a concrete skill or digital tool that an early-career learning designer can apply immediately. The written Try section contextualises and adapts — the video shows. They are companions, not duplicates.
Introduction line
One sentence that names what skill or tool the video demonstrates and why it is worth the viewer's time. Functional — not promotional.
Video block
[Video title]
2–3 sentences of context written to work for both audiences: Substack readers who have not watched yet, and YouTube viewers arriving independently. What will they learn? What will they be able to do afterward that they could not before? Keep this functional.
Playlist line
After the embed or link: "This video is part of the Fieldwork playlist — a standalone series of practical skills for inclusive learning designers." Include the playlist link. This line works both ways: it contextualises the video for Substack readers, and it gives YouTube viewers a path to the full series.
Video design principles
Each video should work without the Substack post — a viewer who finds it on YouTube should understand what they are watching and why without any prior context. Keep videos focused on one skill or tool. Assume early-career practitioners who are capable and busy. Respect their time as much as the post does.
Share The conversation prompt Meaningful progress happens through collaboration
Framing sentence
One sentence that explains why this particular idea benefits from being thought through with others — not generically, but specifically for this week's concept. Make the case for the conversation before offering the prompt.
This week's prompt
A single, specific question or provocation for the reader to bring to a colleague, peer group, or community. Concrete enough to start a real conversation. Open enough that different contexts will produce different answers. Not "discuss accessibility" — something like: "Show someone a piece of learning content you have designed and ask them: what would need to be different for this to work for someone you have not designed for yet?"
Note on community
The Share prompt reflects the conviction that collective knowledge is more resilient than individual expertise. It is not a social media call-to-action — it is an invitation to the kind of conversation that changes how someone sees their work. Write it accordingly.
Share section: 80–120 words
Reflect The open question Points to the field's future, not the post's past
What this section does
Closes the post with a question that is genuinely unresolved — not a summary of what was covered, not a question the post has already answered. It points toward where the field is heading: what is not yet known, what practitioners are still working out, what the reader might be positioned to contribute to. It should feel slightly larger than this week, pointing toward the horizon.
Tone
Curious, not provocative. The question should feel like an invitation to think, not a challenge to answer. It is intended for ongoing consideration, not quick resolution. A reader should be able to return to it weeks later and find it still open.
